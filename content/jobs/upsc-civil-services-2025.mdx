## UPSC Civil Services 2025 - The Exam That Changes Everything

There's no way to sugarcoat this: UPSC CSE is the toughest exam in India. Period. About 10-12 lakh candidates fill the form every year, roughly 5-6 lakh actually show up for Prelims, and around 1,000 make it to the final list. Those odds would scare anyone, and they should. But every year, people from the most ordinary backgrounds crack this exam and go on to become IAS, IPS, and IFS officers. That's why lakhs of aspirants keep trying.

The 2025 notification is out with 1,056 vacancies. UPSC has kicked off the cycle and if you've been preparing, this is your shot.

Everyone wants IAS or IPS, naturally. But look at IRS (Income Tax) with 150 posts - that's a solid service with good perks and a respectable career trajectory. A lot of candidates who don't get IAS/IPS end up in services like IRAS, IAAS, or IPoS and find genuinely fulfilling careers. Don't go in with tunnel vision for just the top three services.

## Who Can Apply

**Age:** 21 to 32 years as on 1st August 2025. SC/ST get 5 years relaxation, OBC (NCL) gets 3 years, PwD gets 10 years.

**Education:** Any bachelor's degree from a recognized university. Even final-year students can apply provisionally.

### How Many Attempts Do You Get?

This is something every aspirant thinks about deeply:

- **General:** 6 attempts
- **OBC (Non-Creamy Layer):** 9 attempts
- **SC/ST:** Unlimited attempts (till age limit)
- **PwD (General):** 9 attempts
- **PwD (OBC):** 9 attempts
- **PwD (SC/ST):** Unlimited (till age limit)

Six attempts sounds like a lot until you've used three and the pressure starts mounting. Every extra year costs money - rent in Delhi, coaching fees, books, test series. The emotional toll is real. Families start asking questions, friends move on with careers and marriages, and you're still in Rajinder Nagar trying to crack the next Prelims. This exam changes lives, but it also breaks many. Go in with a backup plan. That's not being defeatist - it's being smart.

## The Exam Structure

### Prelims - The Great Filter

**Paper I - General Studies:**

| Topic | Approximate Questions |
|-------|----------------------|
| Current Events - National & International | 15-20 |
| History of India and National Movement | 15-18 |
| Indian and World Geography | 12-15 |
| Indian Polity and Governance | 15-18 |
| Economic and Social Development | 10-12 |
| Environment, Ecology, Biodiversity | 8-10 |
| General Science | 10-12 |
| **Total** | **100 Questions / 200 Marks** |

Duration: 2 hours. Negative marking of 1/3rd per wrong answer.

Prelims is a minefield. One bad paper and you're done for the year. The cutoff fluctuates wildly - some years it's 92, some years it's 105+. Current affairs and Polity tend to carry the most weight, but UPSC throws curveballs from Environment and Science sections too. Don't skip any area.

**Paper II - CSAT:**
- 80 questions, 200 marks, 2 hours
- Covers Comprehension, Reasoning, Decision-Making, Basic Numeracy, Data Interpretation
- **Qualifying paper** - you just need 33% (66 marks)

CSAT being qualifying is a relief for humanities students, but don't take it lightly. Every year some otherwise brilliant candidates fail to score 66 in CSAT. Do at least 5-6 mock papers just to be safe. Maths-phobia has cost more aspirants their attempt than you'd think.

### Mains - The Real Exam

| Paper | Subject | Marks |
|-------|---------|-------|
| Paper A | Compulsory Indian Language | 300 (Qualifying) |
| Paper B | English | 300 (Qualifying) |
| Paper I | Essay | 250 |
| Paper II | GS I (History, Geography, Society) | 250 |
| Paper III | GS II (Governance, Polity, Constitution) | 250 |
| Paper IV | GS III (Economy, Technology, Environment) | 250 |
| Paper V | GS IV (Ethics, Integrity, Aptitude) | 250 |
| Paper VI | Optional Subject Paper I | 250 |
| Paper VII | Optional Subject Paper II | 250 |
| **Total (For Merit)** | | **1750** |

Mains is where the exam truly happens. You're writing for 5 days, multiple papers of 3 hours each. It's physically and mentally exhausting.

**The optional subject** is probably the single biggest strategic decision you'll make. Popular choices are Sociology, Geography, PSIR, and Public Administration because of relatively predictable syllabi and good scoring potential. Anthropology used to be a topper's favourite but scoring has become unpredictable. If you have a strong technical background, Maths or Physics optional can give you an edge because competition is low, but the papers are genuinely hard. Choose based on your genuine interest and the availability of good guidance - don't just follow the herd.

**Answer writing** is everything in Mains. You could know the content inside out, but if you can't present it in a structured, time-bound format with diagrams and examples, you'll get average marks. Start answer writing practice at least 4-5 months before Mains. Join a test series from Vision IAS, Insights, ForumIAS, or any reputed platform.

### Interview / Personality Test

- 275 marks
- Conducted by a UPSC board with a chairman and 4-5 members
- Lasts about 25-30 minutes

The interview is unpredictable and that's by design. They'll ask about your DAF (Detailed Application Form) - your hobbies, your home state, your educational background, current events, ethical dilemmas, and sometimes completely random questions. A candidate who listed "cooking" as a hobby was once asked about the chemistry of fermentation. Be genuine in your DAF - don't write hobbies you can't talk about for 10 minutes.

### Final Merit
Main Exam (1750) + Interview (275) = **2025 total marks**

## The Delhi Coaching Hub Reality

Rajinder Nagar and Old Rajinder Nagar in Delhi have been the nerve centre of UPSC preparation for decades. Vajiram & Ravi, Vision IAS, Shankari IAS Academy, GS Score - the big names are all there. A single classroom course costs Rs. 1-2 lakh, and that's before you add rent (Rs. 8,000-15,000/month for a shared PG), food, books, and test series.

Is coaching necessary? Honestly, it depends. Plenty of people have cracked UPSC through self-study with YouTube lectures (Mrunal, StudyIQ) and free online resources. But coaching provides structure, a peer group, and regular evaluation through test series that self-study sometimes can't replicate. If you can afford it, a test series is almost non-negotiable. If you can't afford full coaching, the free resources available today are genuinely excellent.

## How to Apply

1. Go to [upsc.gov.in](https://upsc.gov.in)
2. Find the CSE 2025 notification under "Online Application"
3. Complete Part-I Registration with basic details
4. Save your Registration ID
5. Move to Part-II - fill educational details, optional subject, exam centre preferences
6. Upload photo and signature as per specs
7. Pay Rs. 100 (free for women and SC/ST/PwD) via SBI net banking, card, or at any SBI branch
8. Review, submit, and download the confirmation page

## Preparation Essentials - The Non-Negotiables

Start with NCERTs. Class 6 to 12 for History, Geography, Polity, Economics, and Science. This is the foundation that every topper talks about and there's no shortcut past it.

Standard references: Laxmikanth for Polity (read it cover to cover, twice), Ramesh Singh for Economy, Spectrum for Modern History, Shankar IAS Environment booklet, and Goh Cheng Leong for World Geography.

Read The Hindu or Indian Express daily. Not the entire paper - focus on the editorial, national news, economy section, and anything related to governance. Make short notes. Current affairs is the thread that runs through every paper in both Prelims and Mains.

And revise. Then revise again. The volume of syllabus is so large that you will forget things. Monthly revision of your notes is not optional - it's the difference between scoring 100 in GS and scoring 130.
